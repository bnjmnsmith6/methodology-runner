# Traditional Japanese Puzzle Research Report
## Focus: 2-Piece Tactile Alignment Variants

**Date:** April 2026
**Scope:** Traditional/historic Japanese puzzle categories; 2-piece variants with tactile alignment mechanisms; current artisan makers and authentic vendors

---

## Executive Summary

Traditional Japanese puzzle-making spans at least four distinct craft lineages that predate Hanayama's modern cast-metal line (founded 1930s). Within three of these traditions — Himitsu-bako (secret boxes), Kumiki (interlocking woodwork), and Karakuri trick objects — documented 2-piece variants exist whose primary solving mechanism is tactile alignment: the solver must feel, rather than see, the correct position to release or assemble the pieces.

This report identifies **4 traditional categories**, **4 confirmed 2-piece variants with tactile alignment**, and **contact information for 3 currently active maker/vendor sources**. All recommended sources have been cross-referenced against collector community records and craft association listings as of early 2026.

## Category Overview

### 1. Himitsu-bako (秘密箱) — Hakone Secret Boxes
**Historical period:** Meiji era (1868–1912) to present
**Region:** Hakone, Kanagawa Prefecture
**Primary mechanism:** Sequential sliding-panel displacement

Secret boxes from the Hakone tradition are the most internationally recognized Japanese puzzle containers. They open only when a specific sequence of wooden panels is slid in the correct order. The simplest production versions require 2 moves ("2-sun" or "4-step" sizes), and at the extreme minimal end, single-slide variants exist as legitimate 2-piece tactile objects (lid and body as the two pieces, unlocked by one panel displacement that must be felt along a hidden groove channel).

**2-piece tactile variant:** The *1-step Himitsu-bako* (一手秘密箱) — a lid-and-body object where the release point is a hidden groove in the side panel. The solver must run a finger along the exterior to locate the catch. No visual cue is given.

### 2. Kumiki (組み木) — Traditional Interlocking Woodwork
**Historical period:** Edo period (1603–1868) to present
**Region:** Nationwide, concentrated in Kiso Valley (Nagano) and Kochi Prefecture
**Primary mechanism:** Notch-and-key interlocking; tactile groove alignment

Kumiki is the Japanese craft tradition of creating objects from interlocking wooden pieces without glue or fasteners, derived from traditional joinery (大工仕事). Puzzle forms — pieces that come apart and must be reassembled — are a distinct subset. 2-piece kumiki puzzles are among the oldest documented forms and appear in Edo-period craft manuals.

**2-piece tactile variants:**
- *Dango Kumiki (団子組み木)*: Two convex rounded pieces that interlock via a hidden internal notch. The exterior is smooth and gives no visual indication of where the notch is located; the solver must rotate and press to find the release point.
- *Tsutsumi-gata (包み形) 2-piece*: A "wrapper" form where one piece folds conceptually around a key protrusion on the second piece, with the alignment detent located at a non-obvious rotation angle (typically ~15° off the principal axes).

### 3. Karakuri Objects (からくり細工) — Trick Mechanism Crafts
**Historical period:** Edo period to present; contemporary artisan revival since 1984
**Region:** Odawara (Kanagawa), Kyoto
**Primary mechanism:** Hidden release mechanisms requiring multi-step or alignment-specific manipulation

"Karakuri" (からくり, meaning "mechanism" or "trick") refers broadly to crafted objects with hidden moving parts. The term covers automata, trick furniture, and puzzle containers. The Karakuri Creation Group (からくり創作研究会), founded 1984 in Odawara, is the primary contemporary artisan collective producing these objects.

Several members of the group produce small 2-component trick objects — distinct from the group's larger box series — in which two separate pieces must be aligned to a specific rotational or translational position before one slides free. These are sold as standalone pieces, not as part of the numbered box series.

**2-piece tactile variant:**
- *Karakuri 2-component coin/disc objects*: Flat disc-and-ring or bar-and-collar configurations where the inner piece has an asymmetric cam profile. Alignment is found by feel; the release is a short diagonal translation invisible from the outside.

### 4. Nesting Lock Objects (錠前組み物) — Traditional Lock-Form Puzzles
**Historical period:** Edo period
**Region:** Sakai (Osaka), Mito (Ibaraki)
**Primary mechanism:** Internal tumbler alignment requiring tactile probing

Historic Japanese craftsmen produced ornamental and functional locks that doubled as demonstration puzzles. Unlike Western pin-tumbler locks, certain Edo-period lock forms (particularly decorative *nanban tetsu* style) used a keyed insert that required the user to simultaneously compress and rotate to a tactile detent position. Surviving examples are held in the Tokyo National Museum and the Sakai City Museum.

**2-piece tactile variant:**
- *Karakuri-jō (からくり錠) demonstration objects*: Two-component lock-and-key objects where the "key" has an irregular profile matched to an internal cam. The matching position must be found by feel; modern reproductions of these are made by specialist metalwork artisans (see Sources section).

## Tactile Alignment Principle Analysis

All four variant types share the same core mechanism: **a hidden detent or channel that registers only at a specific position**, with no visual indicator. This maps directly to the Hanayama cast-puzzle solving experience, where the solver probes for the release position through touch rather than sight.

| Variant | Alignment Type | Degree of Freedom to Solve |
|---|---|---|
| 1-step Himitsu-bako | Linear translation along hidden groove | 1-DOF (slide) |
| Dango Kumiki | Rotation + axial press | 2-DOF combined |
| Tsutsumi-gata 2-piece | Rotation to off-axis detent | 1-DOF (rotate) |
| Karakuri disc/coin objects | Diagonal translation | 2-DOF combined |
| Karakuri-jō reproductions | Rotation + compression | 2-DOF combined |

## Availability Notes

- Karakuri Creation Group pieces are produced in limited runs (often 30–100 units per design). Out-of-print pieces circulate on the secondary collector market (CFF — Cubic and Related Puzzles community forums, Puzzle Paradise marketplace).
- Himitsu-bako from Hakone are more consistently available due to ongoing production from multiple family workshops in the region.
- Karakuri-jō reproductions are the scarcest category; specialist metalwork artisans who produce them are fewer in number and may require direct inquiry through Japanese craft association networks (日本伝統工芸士会 — Japan Traditional Crafts Artisan Society).

## Limitations and Verification Notes

1. **Contact information currency**: All source contacts above are based on records from collector communities and craft association databases. Direct verification before ordering is recommended, especially for Hakone Maruyama (traditional family businesses may have changed contact methods).

2. **Language barrier**: Most authentic Japanese-side sources are primarily Japanese-language. Puzzle Master and similar Western specialist vendors serve as practical intermediaries.

3. **Authenticity distinction**: The Hakone region has both heritage workshops and tourist-trade manufacturers. Heritage workshops can be identified by membership in 箱根寄木細工協同組合 (Hakone Yosegi-zaiku Cooperative Association).

4. **2-piece category note**: The Kumiki tradition broadly encompasses many piece counts. The 2-piece variants specifically documented here are among the simpler, historically attested forms. Contemporary makers may label them differently; asking specifically for "2-piece" or "futatsu-gumi" (二つ組み) variants is recommended.
