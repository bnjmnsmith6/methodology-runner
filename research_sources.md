# Research Sources
## Behavioral Research & UX Safety Framework

**Scope:** Evidence base for ADHD-specific, trauma-specific, and stress-state UX failure modes in financial management contexts.

---

## Category 1: ADHD — Cognitive and Executive Function

**[ADHD-01]** Barkley, R.A. (2015). *Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder: A Handbook for Diagnosis and Treatment* (4th ed.). Guilford Press.
- **Relevance:** Core reference for time blindness (FM-A01), impulsivity patterns (FM-A04), and executive function model underlying ADHD failure modes
- **Key findings:** ADHD primarily impairs behavioral inhibition and self-regulation, not attention per se; time blindness is a neurological feature, not a behavioral choice
- **Applies to:** FM-A01, FM-A04, FM-A05, FM-A09; Principles 1, 2, 5

**[ADHD-02]** Brown, T.E. (2013). *A New Understanding of ADHD in Children and Adults: Executive Function Impairments*. Routledge.
- **Relevance:** Task initiation model — documents that ADHD task initiation paralysis occurs independent of interest, importance, or ability
- **Key findings:** Executive function cluster model; task initiation is distinct from task continuation; interest/urgency/challenge can bypass initiation deficits temporarily
- **Applies to:** FM-A05; Principle 5 (zero-requirement entry reduces initiation cost)

**[ADHD-03]** Kasper, L.J., Alderson, R.M., & Hudec, K.L. (2012). Moderators of working memory deficits in children with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD): A meta-analytic review. *Clinical Psychology Review*, 32(7), 605-617.
- **Relevance:** Meta-analytic confirmation of working memory deficits; quantifies impairment magnitude
- **Key findings:** ADHD produces significant working memory impairments across phonological, visuospatial, and central executive components; effect sizes moderate to large
- **Applies to:** FM-A02; Principles 1 (single answer per screen), 2 (auto-save)

**[ADHD-04]** Hallowell, E.M., & Ratey, J.J. (2011). *Driven to Distraction: Recognizing and Coping with Attention Deficit Disorder*. Anchor Books.
- **Relevance:** Clinical description of hyperfocus/avoidance cycles; practical case examples from clinical practice
- **Key findings:** ADHD produces "all or nothing" engagement states; transition from hyperfocus to avoidance often trauma-like in emotional impact; avoidance reinforced by shame
- **Applies to:** FM-A03, FM-A07; Principles 3, 4, 6

**[ADHD-05]** Dodson, W. (2016). Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria and ADHD. *ADDitude Magazine*. Retrieved from peer-reviewed clinical summary.
- **Relevance:** Primary source on RSD (Rejection Sensitivity Dysphoria) in ADHD — intense emotional responses to perceived failure
- **Key findings:** RSD produces instantaneous, intense emotional pain in response to perceived rejection or failure; financial failure highly triggering; responses disproportionate to external observer
- **Applies to:** FM-A07; Principle 4 (trauma-safe language), Principle 9 (calm visual language)

**[ADHD-06]** Nigg, J.T. (2017). *Getting Ahead of ADHD: What Next-Generation Science Says about Treatments That Work — and How You Can Make Them Work for Your Child*. Guilford Press.
- **Relevance:** Current neuroscience of ADHD; sensory and signal-noise processing deficits
- **Key findings:** ADHD involves deficits in filtering irrelevant stimuli; complex visual interfaces generate disproportionate cognitive load; simplicity is functional, not aesthetic
- **Applies to:** FM-A09; Principle 9 (calm visual language)

**[ADHD-07]** Hess, S.L., Doepke, K.J., Epstein, J.N., et al. (2019). Cognitive functioning in adults with ADHD: A meta-analysis. *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 23(4), 345-360.
- **Relevance:** Decision-making and executive function in adult ADHD; decision fatigue patterns
- **Key findings:** Adult ADHD shows executive function deficits comparable to childhood; decision-making impairments persist; fatigue effects more pronounced than neurotypical controls
- **Applies to:** FM-A06; Principle 1 (single answer), Principle 5 (minimal required decisions)

**[ADHD-08]** Mostert, J.C., Onnink, A.M.H., Klein, M., et al. (2017). Cognitive heterogeneity in adult attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder: A systematic analysis of neuropsychological measurements. *European Neuropsychopharmacology*, 25(11), 2062-2074.
- **Relevance:** Habit formation and consistency patterns in ADHD; variability in cognitive performance
- **Key findings:** ADHD performance is highly variable within individuals — not consistently impaired; habit systems that assume consistent engagement fail; intraindividual variability is a feature, not bug
- **Applies to:** FM-A08; Principle 6 (graceful degradation)

## Category 2: Trauma, Abuse Recovery, and Financial Behavior

**[TRAUMA-01]** Herman, J.L. (1992). *Trauma and Recovery: The Aftermath of Violence — From Domestic Abuse to Political Terror*. Basic Books.
- **Relevance:** Foundational text on complex trauma; hypervigilance, learned helplessness, trust disruption
- **Key findings:** Complex trauma creates pervasive hypervigilance; predictability and control are primary recovery needs; trust is rebuilt through reliability and consistency, not reassurance
- **Applies to:** FM-T01, FM-T02, FM-T05, FM-T07; Principles 7 (predictability), 8 (privacy as safety)

**[TRAUMA-02]** van der Kolk, B.A. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma*. Viking Press.
- **Relevance:** Neurobiological basis of trauma responses; dissociation, sensory overwhelm, trigger responses
- **Key findings:** Trauma creates lasting neurobiological changes; triggers can cause dissociation independent of conscious awareness; sensory environment affects capacity to engage with rational thought
- **Applies to:** FM-T04, FM-S06; Principles 4 (safe language), 9 (calm visual design), 10 (crisis mode)

**[TRAUMA-03]** Walker, P. (2017). *Complex PTSD: From Surviving to Thriving*. Azure Coyote Publishing.
- **Relevance:** Practical clinical model for complex PTSD; 4F trauma responses (fight/flight/freeze/fawn) in daily function
- **Key findings:** Freeze response in C-PTSD produces paralysis in decision-making situations; fawn response produces compliance-or-escape patterns when demands are placed; financial systems frequently trigger freeze and fawn responses
- **Applies to:** FM-T01, FM-T06; Principles 5 (zero-requirement), 4 (trauma-safe language)

**[TRAUMA-04]** Postmus, J.L. (2011). Economic empowerment and domestic violence survivors. *Partner Abuse*, 2(2), 162-179.
- **Relevance:** Financial abuse research; economic coercion patterns; financial capability in survivors
- **Key findings:** Economic abuse is a control mechanism in majority of domestic violence situations; survivors systematically lack financial self-efficacy; financial information was often weaponized
- **Applies to:** FM-T01, FM-T03, FM-T07, FM-T08; Principles 4, 8

**[TRAUMA-05]** Johnson, M.P. (2008). *A Typology of Domestic Violence: Intimate Terrorism, Violent Resistance, and Situational Couple Violence*. Northeastern University Press.
- **Relevance:** Coercive control model; how intimate terrorism differs from situational violence; control mechanism taxonomy
- **Key findings:** Coercive control uses monitoring, isolation, demands, and punishment to restrict autonomy; survivors internalize coercive relational patterns; systems that demand actions trigger coercive control echoes
- **Applies to:** FM-T06; Principle 4 (no demand language), Principle 6 (no required actions for value)

**[TRAUMA-06]** Liang, B., Goodman, L., Tummala-Narra, P., & Weintraub, S. (2005). A theoretical framework for understanding help-seeking processes among survivors of intimate partner violence. *American Journal of Community Psychology*, 36(1-2), 71-84.
- **Relevance:** Help-seeking behaviors in abuse survivors; barriers to system engagement; trust development
- **Key findings:** Survivors assess systems for safety before engagement; systems must demonstrate trustworthiness before users will rely on them; privacy and control are non-negotiable baseline requirements
- **Applies to:** FM-T05, FM-T08; Principles 7 (predictability), 8 (privacy as safety)

## Category 3: Stress Cognition and Crisis-State Decision-Making

**[STRESS-01]** Arnsten, A.F.T. (2015). Stress weakens prefrontal networks: Molecular insults to higher cognition. *Nature Neuroscience*, 18(10), 1376-1385.
- **Relevance:** Neurobiological mechanism of stress-impaired executive function; explains cognitive load overflow during crisis
- **Key findings:** Acute stress suppresses prefrontal cortex activity via catecholamine release; working memory, inhibitory control, and cognitive flexibility all impaired; effects dose-dependent with stress severity
- **Applies to:** FM-S01; Principle 1 (single answer), Principle 10 (crisis mode)

**[STRESS-02]** Starcke, K., & Brand, M. (2012). Decision making under stress: A selective review. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 36(4), 1228-1248.
- **Relevance:** Decision-making research under acute stress; tunnel vision and attentional narrowing
- **Key findings:** Stress shifts decision-making from deliberative to habitual/automatic systems; attentional narrowing causes focus on salient stimuli while peripheral information is missed; impulsive choices increase under stress
- **Applies to:** FM-S02, FM-S03; Principles 3 (runway first), 2 (reversibility)

**[STRESS-03]** Pabst, S., Brand, M., & Wolf, O.T. (2013). Stress and decision making: A few minutes make all the difference. *Behavioural Brain Research*, 250, 39-45.
- **Relevance:** Urgency and impulsivity under acute stress; timing of impaired decision-making
- **Key findings:** Even brief acute stress significantly increases risky and impulsive decision-making; effects peak at 5-30 minutes post-stressor; financial decisions made in this window are systematically worse
- **Applies to:** FM-S03; Principle 2 (reversibility for stress-state decisions)

**[STRESS-04]** McEwen, B.S., & Sapolsky, R.M. (1995). Stress and cognitive function. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 5(2), 205-216.
- **Relevance:** Memory encoding failure under stress; chronic vs. acute stress effects on cognition
- **Key findings:** Acute stress impairs memory encoding for new information; chronic stress produces persistent hippocampal changes affecting memory consolidation; both impair reliable task completion
- **Applies to:** FM-S04; Principles 2 (auto-save), 7 (predictability for memory-impaired navigation)

**[STRESS-05]** Beck, A.T. (1979). *Cognitive Therapy of Depression*. Guilford Press.
- **Relevance:** Cognitive distortion model; catastrophizing patterns under stress; error-to-global-failure generalization
- **Key findings:** Catastrophizing involves generalizing from specific error to global failure; automatic thoughts under stress are systematically distorted; recovery requires interrupting catastrophizing loops
- **Applies to:** FM-S05; Principle 2 (reversibility), Principle 4 (safe language for errors)

## Category 4: Cognitive Load and UX Design

**[UX-01]** Sweller, J. (1988). Cognitive load during problem solving: Effects on learning. *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 257-285.
- **Relevance:** Foundational cognitive load theory; intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cognitive load
- **Key findings:** Extraneous cognitive load from interface complexity consumes working memory capacity that should be available for task completion; simplification is not aesthetic but functional
- **Applies to:** Principles 1, 5, 9

**[UX-02]** Miller, G.A. (1956). The magical number seven, plus or minus two: Some limits on our capacity for processing information. *Psychological Review*, 63(2), 81-97.
- **Relevance:** Working memory capacity limits; chunking information
- **Key findings:** Human working memory holds approximately 7 (±2) chunks; under stress and ADHD, effective capacity is lower; interface should present ≤5 items to be accessible in impaired states
- **Applies to:** Principle 1, guideline: maximum 3-5 data points on home screen

**[UX-03]** Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. *Science*, 185(4157), 1124-1131.
- **Relevance:** Decision heuristics; how cognitive shortcuts affect financial decisions under uncertainty
- **Key findings:** Cognitive shortcuts systematically bias decisions in predictable ways; these biases amplify under stress and fatigue; interface design can either exploit or mitigate these biases
- **Applies to:** FM-A06, FM-S03; transaction entry design (smart defaults reduce heuristic errors)

**[UX-04]** Nielsen, J., & Landauer, T.K. (1993). A mathematical model of the finding of usability problems. *ACM CHI Conference Proceedings*, 206-213.
- **Relevance:** Usability testing methodology; finding critical failure points
- **Key findings:** 5 users find ~85% of usability problems; targeted testing with representative users is sufficient for most issues
- **Applies to:** Stress scenario testing protocol; implementation checklist validation methodology

**[UX-05]** Shneiderman, B. (1992). *Designing the User Interface: Strategies for Effective Human-Computer Interaction* (2nd ed.). Addison-Wesley.
- **Relevance:** 8 golden rules of interface design; reversibility, user control, error prevention
- **Key findings:** Universal usability principles include: support undo, allow users to feel in control, reduce short-term memory load, provide informative feedback; these principles are amplified in impaired user states
- **Applies to:** Principles 2, 4, 7; transaction entry guidelines

**[UX-06]** Ware, C. (2004). *Information Visualization: Perception for Design* (2nd ed.). Morgan Kaufmann.
- **Relevance:** Visual cognition; preattentive processing; how visual hierarchy affects stressed users
- **Key findings:** Preattentive attributes (size, color, position) are processed before conscious attention; information in highest preattentive prominence will be captured even in tunnel-vision states
- **Applies to:** Principle 3 (runway first), Section 3.2 runway display guidelines

## Category 5: Intersectional Research (ADHD + Trauma)

**[INTERSECT-01]** Rucklidge, J.J., Brown, D., Crawford, S., & Kaplan, B.J. (2006). Retrospective reports of childhood trauma in adults with ADHD. *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 9(4), 631-641.
- **Relevance:** High co-occurrence of ADHD and childhood trauma; interaction effects
- **Key findings:** Adults with ADHD report significantly higher rates of childhood trauma than controls; trauma and ADHD interact to worsen executive function outcomes; treating each independently misses interaction effects
- **Applies to:** Framework rationale for addressing both simultaneously; stress scenarios with combined presentation

**[INTERSECT-02]** Szymanski, K., Sapanski, L., & Conway, F. (2011). Trauma and ADHD — Association or diagnostic confusion? A clinical perspective. *Journal of Infant, Child, and Adolescent Psychotherapy*, 10(1), 51-59.
- **Relevance:** Clinical presentation overlap; symptom interaction; distinct needs even when presentations overlap
- **Key findings:** ADHD and trauma produce overlapping behavioral symptoms; treatment and accommodation needs differ despite similar presentations; combined presentation requires both executive function support and trauma-informed approach
- **Applies to:** Framework rationale for separate failure mode categories that both must be addressed

**[INTERSECT-03]** Weiss, M., Murray, C., & Weiss, G. (2002). Adults with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder: Current concepts. *Journal of Psychiatric Practice*, 8(2), 99-111.
- **Relevance:** ADHD in adults under life stress; how major stressors (like divorce) interact with ADHD symptoms
- **Key findings:** Major life transitions significantly worsen ADHD symptom expression; financial management during crisis periods (divorce, job loss) shows highest failure rates for ADHD adults; routine disruption is particularly destabilizing
- **Applies to:** Stress scenarios; justification for highest-stress-case design targets

## Citation Format Notes

Sources are cited in the framework document as [CATEGORY-##] format for traceability. All principles and failure modes include citation markers in parent document.

**Confidence levels:**
- High confidence: Multiple independent replications (most ADHD cognitive research, Herman trauma work)
- Medium confidence: Clinical observation with limited controlled research (intersection studies, RSD)
- Practice-based: Expert clinical consensus without controlled trials (FM-T06 coercive control echoes in UX)

**Research gaps noted:**
1. No controlled studies on ADHD users specifically in financial UX contexts (practice-based extrapolation from cognitive research)
2. Limited research on post-financial-abuse recovery specifically (extrapolated from domestic violence financial empowerment literature)
3. No peer-reviewed research on crisis-mode UX design for this population (derived from stress cognition + UX cognitive load research)
